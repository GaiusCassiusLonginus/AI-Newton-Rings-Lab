# Teaching Implementation and Assessment Design

This document describes how the AI-assisted Newton's rings experiment can be implemented in an undergraduate teaching laboratory, including workflow design, instructional structure, and assessment strategy.

## 1. Teaching objective

The experiment is designed not only to verify the Newton's rings relation, but also to help students:

- understand experimental uncertainty
- compare manual and digital measurement approaches
- reflect on the role of AI in scientific practice
- develop data interpretation and reasoning skills

---

## 2. Two-stage experimental workflow

### Stage 1: Manual baseline measurement

Students are required to:

- observe the ring pattern directly through the microscope
- identify ring order
- perform manual measurements using the measuring microscope (JCD3)
- calculate the radius of curvature using traditional methods

Purpose:
- build physical intuition
- expose observational uncertainty
- establish a reference result

---

### Stage 2: AI-assisted analysis

Students then:

- capture fringe images using a smartphone
- perform image-based analysis using the provided workflow
- extract ring diameters automatically
- compute the radius of curvature

Purpose:
- reduce repetitive measurement workload
- introduce data-driven analysis
- explore computational tools in experimental physics

---

## 3. Comparison and reflection

Students must compare:

- manual result vs AI-assisted result
- differences in uncertainty sources
- consistency or discrepancy between methods

Required output:

- explanation of differences
- identification of dominant uncertainty sources
- discussion of which method is more reliable and why

---

## 4. Teaching materials (recommended package)

To support classroom implementation, the following materials are recommended:

- Instructor briefing document (setup checklist and common issues)
- Student worksheet (manual measurement record)
- Image acquisition guideline
- Data recording template
- Uncertainty checklist
- Post-lab reflection sheet

---

## 5. Assessment design

### Suggested components

| Component | Description | Weight |
|----------|------------|--------|
| Manual measurement | Completion and correctness of baseline measurement | 20% |
| Data recording | Proper documentation of raw data and images | 15% |
| AI analysis | Correct use of workflow and result extraction | 15% |
| Comparison report | Quality of reasoning and explanation | 30% |
| Uncertainty analysis | Identification and discussion of errors | 10% |
| Reflection | Insight into AI role and limitations | 10% |

---

## 6. Role of AI in teaching

In this implementation, AI is positioned as:

- an assistant for data processing
- a tool for visualization and comparison
- a support for reducing repetitive workload

AI is NOT:

- a replacement for experimental observation
- a source of unquestionable results
- a grading authority

---

## 7. Risk control in classroom use

To avoid common issues:

- require manual baseline before AI use
- retain raw images for verification
- expose intermediate results (not only final outputs)
- require written justification for discrepancies
- discourage blind trust in automated results

---

## 8. Educational value

The combined workflow helps students understand that:

> experimental uncertainty is inherent and cannot be eliminated, only managed.

The integration of AI shifts the nature of uncertainty rather than removing it, providing a deeper perspective on modern experimental practice.

---

## 9. Adaptability

The framework can be adapted to:

- other optical experiments
- different imaging devices
- inquiry-based extensions (liquid medium, multi-wavelength illumination)

without fundamentally changing the pedagogical structure.
